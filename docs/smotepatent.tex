\section*{FIELD OF THE INVENTION}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The present invention relates generally to the field of machine learning and
computer vision, and more particularly to systems and methods for generating
synthetic training images to address class imbalance in image datasets through
the combination of spherical linear interpolation in a learned embedding space
and class-conditional adversarial image decoding.

% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
\section*{BACKGROUND OF THE INVENTION}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Class Imbalance in Machine Learning}

The problem of class imbalance is one of the most pervasive and consequential
challenges encountered in applied machine learning. Class imbalance arises when
the number of training examples belonging to different categories differs by a
substantial factor, often by one or more orders of magnitude. In supervised
classification tasks applied to image data, this imbalance manifests directly
in model performance: classifiers trained on imbalanced corpora exhibit
systematic bias toward majority classes, achieving inflated aggregate accuracy
while producing unacceptable recall on underrepresented categories. This
failure mode has real consequences across medical imaging, where rare pathology
classes are frequently underrepresented; in autonomous driving, where uncommon
road events must nonetheless be recognized reliably; and in industrial quality
control, where defect classes are inherently rare relative to conforming
products.

The conventional approaches to addressing class imbalance fall into two broad
families: sampling-based methods and cost-sensitive learning methods. Cost-
sensitive approaches modify the training objective to penalize misclassification
of minority examples more heavily than majority examples, but they do not alter
the fundamental information content available to the learner and therefore
provide only modest improvement when the minority class is severely
underrepresented. Sampling-based methods, by contrast, directly alter the
composition of the training corpus, either by removing majority-class examples
through undersampling or by replicating or synthesizing minority-class examples
through oversampling. Of these, synthetic oversampling is generally preferred
for image data because it avoids the information loss inherent in undersampling
while introducing new training examples that can improve the generalization
surface of the learned classifier.

\subsection{Limitations of Prior Interpolation-Based Oversampling}

The Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE), introduced by Chawla
and colleagues~\cite{chawla2002}, was a foundational advance in synthetic
oversampling. In its original form, SMOTE operates directly on the raw feature
vectors of minority-class examples, selecting a minority-class instance and one
of its $k$ nearest neighbors within the same class, then synthesizing a new
example at a random position along the linear segment connecting them in the
original feature space. Formally, for two minority examples $\bx_i$ and
$\bx_j$, SMOTE generates a synthetic example as $\bx_{\text{syn}} = \bx_i +
\lambda(\bx_j - \bx_i)$ where $\lambda$ is drawn uniformly from $[0,1]$.

When SMOTE was originally proposed, application to raw feature vectors of
tabular or low-dimensional data was natural and well-motivated. However, the
application of linear interpolation to image data presents a fundamental
geometric problem that has not been adequately addressed in the prior art. The
space of natural images is not a convex subset of the raw pixel space; rather,
natural images occupy a curved, low-dimensional manifold embedded in a very
high-dimensional ambient pixel space. Linear interpolation between two points
on a curved manifold does not in general remain on the manifold; the resulting
intermediate points may correspond to blurry, physically implausible pixel
arrays that bear no resemblance to any real image. This manifold-departure
problem is particularly severe for image data, where the valid image manifold
is highly nonlinear and where even small deviations from the manifold produce
perceptually unacceptable artifacts.

The inventor has discovered that this limitation is not merely cosmetic but
causes direct degradation in downstream classifier performance. When
interpolated images depart from the valid image manifold, they carry misleading
statistical information about the minority class distribution, and classifiers
trained on such data learn spurious features that do not generalize to test
examples drawn from the true minority distribution. Prior attempts to apply
SMOTE to images in the raw pixel space or in the feature spaces of pre-trained
classifiers have not adequately resolved this fundamental geometric limitation.

\subsection{Limitations of Prior Generative Oversampling Approaches}

An alternative line of prior art employs generative models, particularly
Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)~\cite{goodfellow2014}, to synthesize
minority-class images directly. Under this paradigm, a GAN is trained on
available minority-class examples and then used to generate additional images
that are decoded from random latent vectors. While this approach sidesteps the
manifold-departure problem of direct interpolation, it introduces a distinct
set of limitations.

First, generative approaches that sample from a learned prior distribution do
not provide the structured interpolation property that gives SMOTE its primary
advantage for class-balancing purposes: the ability to fill gaps in the
minority-class sample density in a principled and controlled manner. A GAN
sampling from a broad prior distribution may repeatedly generate examples from
already-well-represented regions of the minority class manifold while failing
to generate examples that fill the specific gaps in the training corpus where
class density is lowest.

Second, GANs trained in isolation on small minority-class corpora frequently
exhibit mode collapse, generating a limited variety of outputs that fail to
capture the full diversity of the minority class distribution. This limitation
is especially acute when the available minority-class training data is small,
which is precisely the regime in which class-balancing is most urgently needed.

Third, prior generative oversampling methods have not established a rigorous
coupling between the interpolation operation in the latent space and the
geometric properties of that space. When the latent space does not have a
well-defined metric structure, interpolation in that space produces results
whose relationship to the true minority-class distribution is unpredictable.
The inventor has observed that this lack of geometric grounding is a root cause
of the unreliability of prior generative oversampling approaches.

\subsection{Absence of Class Conditioning in Prior Decoder Architectures}

A further limitation of prior art approaches to synthetic image generation for
class-balancing is the failure to exploit class identity information within the
generative decoder. In a class-imbalanced dataset, examples belonging to
different classes may occupy well-separated regions of the embedding space.
When a decoder is trained without awareness of which class its input embeddings
belong to, it must learn a single shared generative mapping that spans multiple
class-conditioned distributions. This is particularly problematic when the
decoder architecture employs batch normalization, which computes feature
statistics over batches that may span multiple classes.

The inventor has found that decoding quality degrades substantially when the
decoder is class-unaware, particularly in the fine-grained visual details that
distinguish between similar classes. Class-conditional information, when
properly incorporated, provides the decoder with direct guidance about which
region of the output distribution it should target, enabling more precise and
class-faithful synthetic image generation. Prior art architectures operating on
image embedding spaces have either ignored class-conditional information
entirely or incorporated it through architectural choices that do not provide
sufficient capacity for the decoder to exploit it effectively.

\subsection{Training Instability and Parameter Trajectory Problems}

The training of adversarial image generation systems is well-known to be
unstable, and this instability is particularly pronounced when training is
conducted on the small datasets characteristic of minority-class oversampling
scenarios. The standard GAN training objective involves a minimax game between
a generator and a discriminator, and this game does not have a guaranteed
convergence path for finite-capacity networks trained on finite data. The
resulting training trajectories frequently exhibit mode collapse, oscillation,
and catastrophic forgetting.

One specific manifestation of this instability that has received insufficient
attention in the prior art concerns the temporal trajectory of decoder
parameters during training. It is well established in the optimization
literature that parameter trajectories during stochastic gradient descent
exhibit substantial high-frequency oscillation, and that the time-average of
parameters along such trajectories frequently generalizes better than any
individual point on the trajectory. This property, which underlies classical
methods such as Polyak averaging, is especially important for generative models
where individual training checkpoints may correspond to locally degenerate
solutions. Prior art generative oversampling systems have failed to incorporate
principled parameter averaging schemes, instead using the final training
checkpoint regardless of the quality of its generative outputs relative to
other points along the training trajectory.

\subsection{Lack of Universal Dataset Interface}

A practical limitation that has significantly hindered the adoption of prior
art approaches to synthetic image oversampling is the absence of a universal
dataset interface. Most prior art systems require dataset-specific preprocessing
pipelines, class-label enumeration, and image loading code that must be
rewritten for each new application. This requirement creates substantial
engineering overhead, increases the likelihood of data-handling bugs, and
prevents straightforward comparison of results across datasets. The inventor
has recognized that the lack of a universal, convention-based dataset interface
is a significant barrier to practical deployment of synthetic oversampling
systems and has designed the present invention to address this limitation
directly through a convention-over-configuration dataset loading strategy that
requires no dataset-specific code.

\section*{SUMMARY OF THE INVENTION}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

The present invention provides a system and method for generating high-quality,
class-faithful synthetic images for the purpose of addressing class imbalance
in image datasets. The invention addresses the foregoing limitations of the
prior art through a combination of novel architectural and training innovations.

In a first aspect, the invention provides a computer-implemented method
comprising: encoding images from an imbalanced training dataset to unit-norm
embedding vectors on a high-dimensional unit hypersphere using a convolutional
neural network augmented with an L2 normalization head; identifying, for each
minority-class embedding vector, a set of nearest-neighbor embedding vectors
within the same class using an angular distance metric; synthesizing new
embedding vectors by spherical linear interpolation (SLERP) along geodesic arcs
connecting selected pairs of same-class embedding vectors, such that the
synthesized embeddings remain precisely on the unit hypersphere; decoding the
synthesized embedding vectors to synthetic images using a class-conditional
convolutional decoder that concatenates a learned class embedding vector to the
image embedding vector at the decoder input; and training the encoder and
decoder jointly using a phased schedule comprising a reconstruction-only phase
followed by a Wasserstein GAN training phase with gradient penalty and feature
matching regularization.

In a second aspect, the invention provides a system comprising: a convolutional
encoder with an L2 normalization head that maps images to embeddings on
$\mathbb{S}^{D-1}$; a SLERP oversampling module that generates synthetic
embeddings along class-homogeneous geodesic arcs; a class-conditional DCGAN
decoder with incorporated non-local self-attention and concatenated class
embedding; a WGAN-GP discriminator providing gradient-penalized adversarial
training with intermediate-feature extraction for feature matching loss; an
exponential moving average (EMA) shadow parameter manager; and a universal
dataset interface that ingests any folder-structured image dataset.

In a third aspect, the invention provides a non-transitory computer-readable
medium storing instructions that, when executed, cause a processor to perform
the steps of the method described in the first aspect.

\subsection{System Overview}

In the preferred embodiment, the disclosed system comprises six principal
components that cooperate to synthesize class-faithful images for addressing
class imbalance in image datasets. These components are: a convolutional encoder
with an L2 normalization head, a SLERP oversampling module, a class-conditional
DCGAN decoder with non-local self-attention, a WGAN-GP discriminator, an EMA
shadow parameter manager, and a universal dataset interface. The system is
trained end-to-end in a phased schedule and, upon training completion, operates
as a standalone image synthesis engine capable of generating any requested
number of synthetic images for any class in the training dataset.

Referring to FIG.\ 1, the system architecture operates as follows during
training. An input image $\bx \in \RR^{H \times W \times C}$, where $H$ and
$W$ denote spatial dimensions and $C$ denotes the number of channels, is
provided to an encoder $E_{\bphi}$ parameterized by weights $\bphi$. The
encoder maps the input image to a unit-norm embedding vector $\bz \in
\mathbb{S}^{D-1} \subset \RR^D$, where $D$ denotes the embedding dimensionality
and $\mathbb{S}^{D-1}$ denotes the $(D-1)$-dimensional unit hypersphere. The
class label $y \in \{0, 1, \ldots, K-1\}$ associated with the input image,
where $K$ denotes the total number of classes, is separately processed by a
class embedding lookup $\bm{e}_y = \mathbf{E}_c[y] \in \RR^{d_c}$, where
$\mathbf{E}_c \in \RR^{K \times d_c}$ is a learnable class embedding matrix
and $d_c$ is the class embedding dimensionality.

The unit-norm embedding $\bz$ and the class embedding $\bm{e}_y$ are
concatenated to form the conditioning vector $\bc = [\bz; \bm{e}_y] \in
\RR^{D + d_c}$, which is provided to a class-conditional DCGAN decoder
$G_{\btheta}$ parameterized by weights $\btheta$. The decoder reconstructs the
input image as $\hat{\bx} = G_{\btheta}(\bc)$. A WGAN-GP discriminator
$D_{\bpsi}$ parameterized by weights $\bpsi$ receives either real images $\bx$
or reconstructed images $\hat{\bx}$ and produces a real-valued critic score.
During training, the adversarial loss, reconstruction loss, and feature matching
loss are computed and used to update the parameters $\btheta$, $\bphi$, and
$\bpsi$ according to the phased training schedule described herein. An EMA
manager maintains shadow parameters $\tilde{\btheta}$ that track the
time-averaged decoder weights throughout training.

Upon completion of training, the SLERP oversampling module operates on the
encoded representations of the training data. For each minority class $k$, the
module constructs a set of synthesized embeddings $\{\hat{\bz}_i\}$ by applying
the SLERP formula along geodesic arcs connecting selected pairs of same-class
real embedding vectors. These synthesized embeddings, together with their
associated class labels, are then passed through the EMA-weight decoder
$G_{\tilde{\btheta}}$ to produce synthetic images. These synthetic images are
added to the training corpus to address the class imbalance.

\subsection{The Convolutional Encoder with L2 Normalization Head}

\subsubsection{Encoder Architecture}

In the preferred embodiment, the encoder $E_{\bphi}$ is built upon a
ResNet-18 backbone~\cite{he2016} pretrained on ImageNet. The ResNet-18
backbone comprises four residual block groups, each group consisting of two
residual blocks with batch normalization and ReLU activations. The four groups
operate on feature maps of spatial resolution $H/8 \times W/8$,
$H/16 \times W/16$, $H/32 \times W/32$, and $H/64 \times W/64$ respectively,
where $H$ and $W$ denote the input spatial dimensions. The number of feature
channels in the four groups is 64, 128, 256, and 512, respectively.

Following the final residual block group, a global average pooling layer reduces
the spatial dimensions to produce a $512$-dimensional feature vector $\bh \in
\RR^{512}$. This feature vector is passed through an L2 normalization head
comprising a linear projection layer followed by an L2 normalization operation.
Specifically, the linear projection is $\bp = \mathbf{W}_{\text{proj}}\,\bh +
\bb_{\text{proj}}$, where $\mathbf{W}_{\text{proj}} \in \RR^{D \times 512}$ and
$\bb_{\text{proj}} \in \RR^D$ are learnable parameters, and the embedding is
obtained by L2 normalization:

\begin{equation}
  \bz = \frac{\bp}{\|\bp\|_2},
  \label{eq:l2norm}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\|\cdot\|_2$ denotes the Euclidean norm. The resulting
embedding $\bz$ satisfies $\|\bz\|_2 = 1$ by construction, i.e., $\bz \in
\mathbb{S}^{D-1}$.

In the preferred embodiment, $D = 512$. In an alternative embodiment, $D$ may
be set to any value in the range $[128, 1024]$ without departing from the scope
of the invention. In yet another alternative embodiment, the encoder backbone
may be replaced by a ResNet-50~\cite{he2016} architecture, which produces a
$2048$-dimensional backbone feature vector prior to projection. The ResNet-50
backbone provides greater representational capacity at the cost of increased
computational expense and may be preferred when the training dataset is large
and diverse.

\subsubsection{Motivation for the L2 Normalization Head}

The L2 normalization head serves a purpose beyond merely constraining the norm
of the embedding vector. The inventor has recognized that unit-norm
normalization induces a geometry on the embedding space that is precisely
compatible with the spherical linear interpolation operation described
hereinafter. When embeddings are constrained to lie on $\mathbb{S}^{D-1}$, the
natural interpolation between two embeddings is the geodesic arc connecting
them on the sphere, and the geodesic arc is precisely what SLERP traces. In the
absence of the L2 normalization constraint, embeddings may have arbitrary norms,
and the SLERP formula does not produce geometrically meaningful interpolants.

Furthermore, the inventor has discovered that L2 normalization has a beneficial
regularizing effect on encoder training, analogous to the effect observed in
metric learning literature. By forcing the encoder to represent all input images
as points on a fixed-radius hypersphere, the normalization prevents the encoder
from using the magnitude of the embedding vector as an additional degree of
freedom, effectively encouraging the encoder to allocate all representational
capacity to the angular structure of the embedding space, which is precisely
the structure that SLERP operates on.

\subsubsection{Alternative Encoder Architectures}

It will be appreciated by those skilled in the art that the ResNet backbone
described above is not the sole architecture suitable for use as the encoder
component. In one alternative embodiment, a Vision Transformer (ViT) backbone
may be substituted for the ResNet backbone, with the class token output of the
transformer serving as the input to the projection head. In another alternative
embodiment, an EfficientNet backbone may be used, providing a favorable
trade-off between accuracy and computational cost. In yet another alternative
embodiment, the encoder may be trained from scratch rather than initialized
from ImageNet pretrained weights, which may be preferred in applications where
the input image domain is substantially different from the ImageNet domain.
Regardless of the choice of backbone, the L2 normalization head described in
Equation~(\ref{eq:l2norm}) is applied to the backbone output in all embodiments
of the present invention.

\subsection{Spherical Linear Interpolation for Manifold-Preserving Oversampling}

\subsubsection{Geometric Motivation}

The fundamental innovation of the oversampling component of the present
invention is the replacement of the standard linear interpolation (LERP) used
in prior art SMOTE implementations with spherical linear interpolation (SLERP).
This replacement is geometrically motivated by the unit-norm constraint imposed
by the L2 normalization head. When embeddings lie on the unit hypersphere
$\mathbb{S}^{D-1}$, straight-line interpolation between two points on the
sphere passes through the interior of the sphere rather than along the sphere
surface, departing from the manifold on which the real data are constrained to
lie. SLERP, by contrast, interpolates along the geodesic arc of $\mathbb{S}^{D-1}$
connecting the two points, ensuring that all interpolated embeddings remain on
the unit sphere.

Referring to FIG.\ 2, the geometric distinction between LERP and SLERP is
illustrated for the case of the two-dimensional unit circle (which generalizes
naturally to the high-dimensional case). Given two unit-norm vectors $\bz_0$
and $\bz_1$ on $\mathbb{S}^{D-1}$, the linear interpolant $\bz_{\text{lerp}}(t)
= (1-t)\bz_0 + t\bz_1$ passes through the interior of the ball for $t \in
(0,1)$, departing from the sphere. The SLERP interpolant $\bz_{\text{slerp}}(t)$
remains on the sphere for all $t \in [0,1]$.

\subsubsection{The SLERP Formula}

The spherical linear interpolation formula is formally defined as follows.
Let $\bz_0, \bz_1 \in \mathbb{S}^{D-1}$ be two unit-norm vectors, and let
$\omega \in [0, \pi]$ denote the angle between them, computed as:

\begin{equation}
  \omega = \arccos\!\bigl(\bz_0 \cdot \bz_1\bigr),
  \label{eq:omega}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\bz_0 \cdot \bz_1$ denotes the inner product of $\bz_0$ and
$\bz_1$. For a given interpolation parameter $t \in [0,1]$, the SLERP
interpolant is:

\begin{equation}
  \hat{\bz}(t) = \frac{\sin\bigl((1-t)\,\omega\bigr)}{\sin\omega}\,\bz_0
                + \frac{\sin\bigl(t\,\omega\bigr)}{\sin\omega}\,\bz_1.
  \label{eq:slerp}
\end{equation}

\noindent It is straightforward to verify that $\|\hat{\bz}(t)\|_2 = 1$ for
all $t \in [0,1]$, confirming that the SLERP interpolant remains on the unit
hypersphere. Furthermore, the SLERP formula provides constant angular velocity
along the geodesic arc, meaning that equal increments in the interpolation
parameter $t$ correspond to equal angular displacements along the arc. This
constant-velocity property is important for oversampling, as it ensures that
the synthetic embeddings are uniformly distributed along the geodesic arc.

\subsubsection{Numerical Stability for Nearly Collinear Vectors}

In practice, the SLERP formula as stated in Equation~(\ref{eq:slerp}) exhibits
numerical instability when $\omega$ is close to zero, i.e., when $\bz_0$ and
$\bz_1$ are nearly collinear. In this degenerate case, $\sin\omega \approx 0$,
and the formula involves division by a near-zero quantity. The inventor has
found that a robust implementation should detect this case and fall back to
spherical normalization of the standard linear interpolant, which closely
approximates SLERP when $\omega \ll 1$. Specifically, for $\omega < \epsilon$
where $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$ in the preferred embodiment, the synthesized
embedding is computed as:

\begin{equation}
  \hat{\bz}(t) = \frac{(1-t)\bz_0 + t\bz_1}{\|(1-t)\bz_0 + t\bz_1\|_2},
  \label{eq:slerp_fallback}
\end{equation}

\noindent which is equivalent to projecting the linear interpolant back onto the
unit sphere. This fallback is applied only in the degenerate near-collinear
case and does not affect the behavior of the algorithm in typical operation.

\subsubsection{Generalized SLERP with $n$-Point Interpolation}

In one alternative embodiment, the SLERP interpolation is generalized to
synthesize embeddings from weighted combinations of more than two anchor points.
For a set of anchor embeddings $\{\bz_i\}_{i=1}^n$ and corresponding
non-negative weights $\{w_i\}_{i=1}^n$ satisfying $\sum_i w_i = 1$, the
generalized spherical interpolant is computed by iterative application of the
two-point SLERP formula as follows. Beginning with $\bz_{\text{cur}} = \bz_1$,
the algorithm iteratively merges each subsequent anchor point:

\begin{equation}
  \bz_{\text{cur}} \leftarrow \text{SLERP}\!\left(\bz_{\text{cur}},\; \bz_{i+1},\;
    \frac{w_{i+1}}{\sum_{j=1}^{i+1} w_j}\right),
  \qquad i = 1, \ldots, n-1.
  \label{eq:slerp_general}
\end{equation}

\noindent This generalized formula allows the synthesis of embeddings that
interpolate among multiple representatives of a class, which may be preferable
when the minority class exhibits multiple sub-clusters. In the preferred
two-point embodiment, $n = 2$ and the generalized formula reduces to
Equation~(\ref{eq:slerp}).

\subsubsection{k-Nearest Neighbor Selection}

For each minority-class embedding $\bz_i$, the SLERP oversampling module
identifies a set of $k$ nearest neighbors within the same class using the
cosine similarity metric, which is equivalent to the angular metric on the
unit sphere. Specifically, for $\bz_0, \bz_1 \in \mathbb{S}^{D-1}$, the cosine
similarity is $\text{sim}(\bz_0, \bz_1) = \bz_0 \cdot \bz_1 = \cos\omega$.
The $k$ nearest neighbors of $\bz_i$ are the $k$ embeddings $\bz_j$,
$j \neq i$, with the highest cosine similarity to $\bz_i$ among all
same-class embeddings. In the preferred embodiment, $k = 5$.

For each synthetic embedding to be generated, the oversampling module selects
one of the $k$ nearest neighbors uniformly at random and draws the
interpolation parameter $t$ uniformly from $(0, 1)$. This procedure ensures
that synthetic embeddings are distributed throughout the gaps in the
minority-class sample density, consistent with the motivation of SMOTE.

\subsubsection{Class Homogeneity Constraint}

A critical aspect of the present invention is the enforcement of class
homogeneity in the SLERP interpolation: the two anchor embeddings $\bz_0$ and
$\bz_1$ used in Equation~(\ref{eq:slerp}) are always drawn from the same class.
This constraint ensures that the synthesized embeddings represent plausible
combinations of same-class image features, rather than cross-class blends that
could introduce misleading label-feature associations. In an alternative
embodiment, the class homogeneity constraint may be partially relaxed to permit
interpolation between embeddings of closely related classes (e.g., different
breeds of the same animal species), provided that the interpolated embeddings
are assigned the label of the embedding from which the nearest-neighbor was
drawn. However, the standard embodiment enforces strict class homogeneity.

\subsection{Class-Conditional DCGAN Decoder}

\subsubsection{Overview of Decoder Architecture}

In the preferred embodiment, the decoder $G_{\btheta}$ is based on the DCGAN
generator architecture introduced by Radford and colleagues~\cite{radford2016},
with several important modifications. The DCGAN architecture uses a sequence
of transposed convolutional layers (also known as fractionally-strided
convolutions) to progressively upsample the conditioning vector to the target
image resolution. The modifications introduced in the present invention include:
incorporation of a learned class embedding that is concatenated to the image
embedding at the decoder input; placement of a non-local self-attention block
at the $16 \times 16$ spatial resolution stage; removal of batch normalization
from all layers; and use of spectral normalization in the decoder.

\subsubsection{Class Embedding and Input Construction}

The decoder input is constructed by concatenating the image embedding
$\bz \in \mathbb{S}^{D-1}$ with the class embedding $\bm{e}_y \in \RR^{d_c}$
to form the conditioning vector $\bc = [\bz;\, \bm{e}_y] \in \RR^{D + d_c}$.
In the preferred embodiment, $d_c = 64$. The class embedding matrix
$\mathbf{E}_c \in \RR^{K \times 64}$ is initialized with values drawn from
$\mathcal{N}(0, 0.01)$ and is jointly optimized with all other decoder
parameters during training.

The inventor has discovered that the concatenation of a learned class embedding
is substantially more effective than one-hot class conditioning for the
following reason. One-hot class conditioning provides a sparse signal of
dimensionality $K$ that contains no notion of class similarity; two distinct
classes are represented as equally distant regardless of their semantic
relationship. A learned class embedding, by contrast, develops a distributed
representation in which semantically similar classes occupy nearby regions of
the class embedding space. This richer representation enables the decoder to
exploit shared structure between related classes, which is particularly
beneficial for cross-class generalization when some classes have few examples.

\subsubsection{Transposed Convolutional Backbone}

The conditioning vector $\bc \in \RR^{D + d_c}$ is first reshaped to a
$4 \times 4$ spatial feature map with $(D + d_c)/16$ channels, and then
passed through a sequence of transposed convolutional layers that progressively
double the spatial resolution while reducing the channel count. In the preferred
embodiment targeting $128 \times 128$ output resolution, the decoder comprises
five transposed convolutional layers operating at spatial resolutions
$4 \times 4$, $8 \times 8$, $16 \times 16$, $32 \times 32$, $64 \times 64$,
and $128 \times 128$. Each transposed convolutional layer uses a $4 \times 4$
kernel with stride 2 and padding 1. The channel counts for the five layers are
512, 256, 128, 64, and the output channel count $C$ (equal to 1 for grayscale
images and 3 for color images), respectively. Spectral normalization is applied
to the weight matrices of each transposed convolutional layer.

In an alternative embodiment, the transposed convolutional layers may be
replaced by resize-convolution blocks, in which a bilinear upsampling operation
is followed by a standard convolutional layer. The inventor has found that
resize-convolution can reduce checkerboard artifacts in some image domains,
though at the cost of some spatial detail. In yet another alternative embodiment,
the output resolution can be varied by adding or removing transposed
convolutional layers, enabling the decoder to target any power-of-two output
resolution from $32 \times 32$ to $512 \times 512$.

\subsubsection{Non-Local Self-Attention Block}

A non-local self-attention block~\cite{zhang2019,wang2018} is inserted in the
decoder at the $16 \times 16$ spatial resolution stage. Referring to FIG.\ 6,
for a feature map $\mathbf{F} \in \RR^{H' \times W' \times C'}$ at the
$16 \times 16$ stage (where $H' = W' = 16$ and $C'$ is the number of channels
at that stage), the self-attention block operates as follows.

Three $1 \times 1$ convolutional projections are applied to $\mathbf{F}$ to
produce query, key, and value tensors:

\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{Q} = \mathbf{W}_Q \mathbf{F}, \quad
  \mathbf{K} = \mathbf{W}_K \mathbf{F}, \quad
  \mathbf{V} = \mathbf{W}_V \mathbf{F},
  \label{eq:qkv}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mathbf{W}_Q, \mathbf{W}_K \in \RR^{(C'/8) \times C'}$ and
$\mathbf{W}_V \in \RR^{C' \times C'}$ are learnable projection matrices.
The attention map $\mathbf{A} \in \RR^{(H'W') \times (H'W')}$ is computed as:

\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{A} = \text{softmax}\!\left(\mathbf{Q}^\top \mathbf{K}\right),
  \label{eq:attn}
\end{equation}

\noindent and the attended feature map is $\mathbf{O} = \mathbf{V}\mathbf{A}$.
The block output is:

\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{F}' = \mathbf{F} + \gamma_{\text{attn}} \cdot \mathbf{O},
  \label{eq:attn_out}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\gamma_{\text{attn}}$ is a scalar parameter initialized to
zero. This zero initialization of $\gamma_{\text{attn}}$ ensures that at the
start of training, the self-attention block contributes nothing to the decoder
output, allowing the training to begin with the local convolutional structure
already established before the long-range attention mechanism gradually learns
to augment it. The inventor has found that this initialization strategy is
critical for stable training with self-attention, as a non-zero initialization
frequently causes the attention mechanism to destabilize the early stages of
training before the convolutional layers have converged to a useful feature
representation.

The non-local self-attention mechanism enables the decoder to model long-range
spatial dependencies, which is important for generating structurally coherent
images in which distant parts of the image must be consistent (e.g., the
symmetric bilateral structure of faces or animals, or the consistent texture
of fur across an entire animal body). Purely local convolutional architectures
cannot model such long-range dependencies without a very deep network, and the
self-attention block provides a computationally efficient mechanism for
capturing them.

The placement of the self-attention block at the $16 \times 16$ spatial
resolution stage is deliberate. At this resolution, the spatial extent of the
feature map is small enough that the attention mechanism is computationally
tractable (the attention map has $(16 \times 16)^2 = 65536$ entries), while
still being large enough to capture meaningful long-range relationships. At
higher resolutions, the quadratic cost of the attention mechanism becomes
prohibitive; at lower resolutions (e.g., $4 \times 4$ or $8 \times 8$), the
spatial structure is too coarse for long-range attention to be meaningful.

In an alternative embodiment, an axial attention mechanism may be used in
place of the full non-local self-attention, decomposing the 2D attention
computation into two sequential 1D attention operations along the row and
column dimensions. This reduces the computational cost from $O((H'W')^2)$ to
$O(H'^2 + W'^2)$ while retaining the long-range dependency modeling capability.
In yet another alternative embodiment, multiple self-attention blocks may be
placed at multiple spatial resolution stages, with the understanding that
computational cost increases proportionally.

\subsubsection{Batch Normalization Replacement}

The standard DCGAN generator architecture uses batch normalization (BN) in each
convolutional layer. The present invention departs from this convention by
removing batch normalization from all decoder layers. The inventor has observed
that batch normalization introduces complex interactions between samples in a
training minibatch, particularly when the minibatch contains examples from
multiple classes with distinct feature statistics. In the class-conditional
decoder, the class embedding already provides class-specific modulation of the
feature representations, and batch normalization applied on top of this can
partially cancel the class-specific modulation by re-normalizing the features
to a common batch-level distribution.

Furthermore, batch normalization has been shown in the GAN literature to be a
contributing factor to training instability, particularly in the WGAN training
regime. By removing batch normalization and replacing it with spectral
normalization of the weight matrices, the present invention achieves more
stable training dynamics without sacrificing the regularization benefits of
normalization.

In an alternative embodiment, conditional batch normalization (CBN) may be
used, in which the scale and shift parameters of the batch normalization are
predicted from the class embedding rather than being fixed parameters. This
allows the normalization statistics to be class-conditional, providing the
class-specific modulation without the negative interaction described above.

\subsection{The WGAN-GP Discriminator}

\subsubsection{Discriminator Architecture}

In the preferred embodiment, the discriminator $D_{\bpsi}$ is a
Wasserstein-distance critic~\cite{arjovsky2017,gulrajani2017} implemented as
a fully convolutional network with strided convolutions. The discriminator
architecture is the mirror of the decoder in terms of spatial downsampling: it
comprises five strided convolutional layers that progressively halve the spatial
resolution and double the channel count, followed by a global average pooling
layer and a linear output layer producing a scalar critic score. The channel
counts for the five convolutional layers mirror those of the decoder in reverse
order. No sigmoid activation is applied to the output, consistent with the
Wasserstein distance critic formulation.

In the Wasserstein GAN framework~\cite{arjovsky2017}, the discriminator
functions as a critic estimating the Wasserstein distance between the
distribution of real images and the distribution of generated images. The
Wasserstein distance provides a more informative training signal than the
Jensen-Shannon divergence used in the original GAN formulation, particularly
when the two distributions have disjoint or near-disjoint supports. Crucially
for the present application, the Wasserstein distance critic does not saturate
as the generated distribution diverges from the real distribution, providing
useful gradients throughout the early stages of training when the generated
images are of low quality.

\subsubsection{Gradient Penalty}

To enforce the Lipschitz constraint required for the Wasserstein distance
interpretation, the present invention uses the gradient penalty (GP) approach
of Gulrajani and colleagues~\cite{gulrajani2017} rather than the weight
clipping approach of the original WGAN. The gradient penalty loss is defined as:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{GP}} = \lambda_{\text{GP}}\,
    \EE_{\hat{\bx}}\!\left[\bigl(\|\nabla_{\hat{\bx}} D_{\bpsi}(\hat{\bx})\|_2 - 1\bigr)^2\right],
  \label{eq:gp}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\hat{\bx} = \epsilon\,\bx_{\text{real}} + (1 - \epsilon)\,\bx_{\text{fake}}$
with $\epsilon \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1]$ is a random convex interpolation
between a real image and a generated image, and $\lambda_{\text{GP}} = 10$
in the preferred embodiment. The gradient penalty enforces the 1-Lipschitz
constraint on the discriminator by penalizing deviations of the gradient norm
from unity on interpolated samples.

The inventor has found that the gradient penalty approach is substantially more
stable than weight clipping in the context of the present application, where
the small minority-class datasets make training stability particularly
important. Weight clipping can cause the discriminator to learn degenerate
critic functions that do not accurately reflect the Wasserstein distance,
whereas the gradient penalty approach empirically produces well-behaved critics
even on small datasets.

\subsubsection{Absence of Batch Normalization in Discriminator}

Consistent with the recommendation of Gulrajani and colleagues~\cite{gulrajani2017}
and the analysis of Miyato and colleagues~\cite{miyato2018}, the discriminator
in the present invention uses no batch normalization. Batch normalization in
the discriminator is incompatible with the gradient penalty, because the
gradient penalty requires computing gradients of the discriminator output with
respect to the input, and batch normalization makes these gradients depend on
all other samples in the batch in a way that violates the 1-Lipschitz
constraint. The discriminator instead uses spectral normalization of weight
matrices to regularize the Lipschitz constant of the discriminator.

\subsubsection{Intermediate Feature Extraction for Feature Matching}

In addition to the scalar critic score, the discriminator provides intermediate
feature maps extracted at the output of each strided convolutional layer. These
intermediate features are used in computing the feature matching loss described
in the following section. The intermediate feature extraction pathway is
implemented by registering forward hooks on the selected discriminator layers
and caching the resulting feature tensors. In the preferred embodiment, features
are extracted from the outputs of all five strided convolutional layers.

In one alternative embodiment, the discriminator may be replaced by a
projection discriminator~\cite{miyato2018}, in which the class information is
incorporated into the discriminator through an inner product between the class
embedding and a discriminator intermediate representation. This modification
makes the discriminator class-conditional rather than class-unconditional,
which may improve the ability of the discriminator to distinguish
class-conditional quality defects from class-unconditional defects.

\subsection{Phased Training with Multiple Loss Functions}

\subsubsection{Overview of Phased Training Schedule}

The inventor has discovered that training the encoder-decoder system directly
with the full adversarial objective from the start of training frequently leads
to training instability, particularly on small datasets. The problem is that
the adversarial objective requires the discriminator to provide a meaningful
training signal to the decoder, which is only possible once the decoder is
generating images of sufficient quality that the discriminator can learn to
distinguish them from real images. If the decoder is initialized to random
weights and immediately subjected to adversarial training, the resulting
training signal is often dominated by noise, and the decoder may fail to
learn any useful image structure.

The present invention addresses this problem through a phased training schedule
that divides the total training duration of $T$ epochs into two phases. In
Phase 1, occupying epochs $0$ through $\lfloor 0.3\,T \rfloor$, training is
conducted exclusively with the reconstruction loss, allowing the encoder and
decoder to establish a meaningful reconstruction mapping before adversarial
training begins. In Phase 2, occupying epochs $\lfloor 0.3\,T \rfloor$ through
$T$, the full adversarial objective is activated and the Wasserstein GAN
training proceeds. This phased schedule ensures that the decoder produces
images of sufficient quality by the start of Phase 2 that the discriminator
can provide a meaningful and stable training signal throughout adversarial
training.

Referring to FIG.\ 8, the phased training schedule is illustrated as a
timeline showing the active loss terms in each phase. In Phase 1, only the
reconstruction loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}}$ is active. In Phase 2, the
reconstruction loss, the WGAN generator loss $\mathcal{L}_G$, the gradient
penalty loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{GP}}$, and the feature matching loss
$\mathcal{L}_{\text{FM}}$ are all active.

\subsubsection{Reconstruction Loss}

In the preferred embodiment, the reconstruction loss $\mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}}$
is a combination of a pixel-level L1 reconstruction term and a VGG-16
perceptual loss term~\cite{johnson2016}. The pixel-level L1 term is:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{L1}} = \EE_{\bx \sim p_{\text{data}}}\!\bigl[\|\bx - G_{\btheta}([\bz;\,\bm{e}_y])\|_1\bigr],
  \label{eq:l1}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\bz = E_{\bphi}(\bx)$ is the unit-norm embedding of $\bx$ and
$\bm{e}_y$ is the class embedding for class $y$.

The VGG-16 perceptual loss~\cite{simonyan2015,johnson2016} computes the
discrepancy between the input image and the reconstruction in the feature space
of a pretrained VGG-16 network. Specifically, the perceptual loss is:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{perc}} = \sum_{\ell \in \mathcal{L}_{\text{vgg}}}
    \EE_{\bx}\!\left[\bigl\|\phi_\ell(\bx) - \phi_\ell(G_{\btheta}([\bz;\,\bm{e}_y]))\bigr\|_1\right],
  \label{eq:perc}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\phi_\ell(\cdot)$ denotes the feature map extracted from layer
$\ell$ of the pretrained VGG-16 network, and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{vgg}}$ is the
set of VGG-16 layers from which features are extracted. In the preferred
embodiment, $\mathcal{L}_{\text{vgg}}$ comprises the outputs of VGG-16 layers
\texttt{relu1\_2}, \texttt{relu2\_2}, \texttt{relu3\_3}, and \texttt{relu4\_3}.
The combined reconstruction loss is:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}} = \lambda_{\text{L1}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{L1}}
    + \lambda_{\text{perc}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{perc}},
  \label{eq:recon}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\lambda_{\text{L1}} = 10.0$ and $\lambda_{\text{perc}} = 1.0$
in the preferred embodiment. The perceptual loss encourages the reconstruction
to be perceptually similar to the input image in terms of high-level feature
content, rather than merely achieving low pixel-level error. Pixel-level losses
such as L1 and L2 are known to produce blurry reconstructions because they
optimize the mean over the distribution of plausible images, whereas perceptual
losses encourage the reconstruction to have sharp, coherent features.

\subsubsection{Wasserstein GAN Losses}

The adversarial training in Phase 2 uses the Wasserstein GAN objective. The
discriminator loss is:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_D = -\EE_{\bx \sim p_{\text{data}}}\!\bigl[D_{\bpsi}(\bx)\bigr]
    + \EE_{\bz \sim p(\bz)}\!\bigl[D_{\bpsi}(G_{\btheta}(\bz))\bigr]
    + \mathcal{L}_{\text{GP}},
  \label{eq:disc}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{GP}}$ is the gradient penalty defined in
Equation~(\ref{eq:gp}). The generator loss is:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_G = -\EE_{\bz \sim p(\bz)}\!\bigl[D_{\bpsi}(G_{\btheta}(\bz))\bigr].
  \label{eq:gen}
\end{equation}

In Phase 2, the discriminator is updated $n_{\text{disc}} = 5$ times for every
generator update. This asymmetric update ratio is standard practice in WGAN
training and ensures that the discriminator maintains a sufficiently tight
approximation of the Wasserstein distance before each generator update.

\subsubsection{Feature Matching Loss}

The feature matching loss~\cite{salimans2016} is an additional regularization
term that encourages the generator to produce images whose statistical
properties in the discriminator's intermediate feature space match those of
the real images. The feature matching loss is:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{FM}} = \sum_{\ell \in \mathcal{L}_{\text{disc}}}
    \EE\!\left[\bigl\|\mathbf{f}_\ell(\bx_{\text{real}}) - \mathbf{f}_\ell(\bx_{\text{fake}})\bigr\|_1\right],
  \label{eq:fm}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mathbf{f}_\ell(\cdot)$ denotes the feature map extracted at
layer $\ell$ of the discriminator and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{disc}}$ is the set of
discriminator layers from which features are extracted. In the preferred
embodiment, features are extracted from all five strided convolutional layers
of the discriminator.

The feature matching loss serves two purposes. First, it provides a training
signal to the generator that is complementary to the Wasserstein adversarial
signal, encouraging the generator to match the distributional properties of
real images at multiple levels of abstraction simultaneously. Second, it
stabilizes training by preventing the generator from exploiting the
discriminator's blind spots, i.e., aspects of the image distribution that the
discriminator has not yet learned to evaluate accurately.

\subsubsection{Combined Generator Training Objective}

The complete generator training objective in Phase 2 is:

\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = \lambda_G\,\mathcal{L}_G
    + \lambda_{\text{recon}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}}
    + \lambda_{\text{FM}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{FM}},
  \label{eq:total}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\lambda_G = 1.0$, $\lambda_{\text{recon}} = 1.0$, and
$\lambda_{\text{FM}} = 10.0$ in the preferred embodiment. The inclusion of the
reconstruction loss in Phase 2 as well as Phase 1 serves as a regularizer that
prevents the decoder from drifting too far from reconstruction fidelity in
pursuit of adversarial quality. The inventor has found that without this
regularization, adversarial training can cause the decoder to generate images
that look realistic to the discriminator but no longer faithfully represent the
content of the input embedding.

\subsubsection{Optimizer Configuration}

Both the encoder-decoder system and the discriminator are optimized with the
Adam optimizer~\cite{kingma2015}. In the preferred embodiment, the Adam
hyperparameters are $\beta_1 = 0.5$ and $\beta_2 = 0.999$, with a learning
rate of $\eta = 0.0002$ for the encoder-decoder system and $\eta = 0.0002$ for
the discriminator. The choice of $\beta_1 = 0.5$ (rather than the more common
$\beta_1 = 0.9$) is standard practice for GAN training~\cite{radford2016}, as
it reduces the momentum of the first-order moment estimate and thus reduces the
tendency of GAN training to overshoot and oscillate.

\subsubsection{Learning Rate Schedule}

A cosine annealing schedule~\cite{loshchilov2017} is applied to the learning
rates of both optimizers. The cosine annealing schedule reduces the learning
rate from its initial value $\eta_0$ to a minimum value $\eta_{\min}$ over the
course of training according to:

\begin{equation}
  \eta_t = \eta_{\min} + \frac{1}{2}(\eta_0 - \eta_{\min})
    \left(1 + \cos\!\left(\frac{t}{T}\,\pi\right)\right),
  \label{eq:lr}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $t$ is the current epoch and $T$ is the total number of
training epochs. In the preferred embodiment, $\eta_{\min} = 0$ and $T$ is
provided as a hyperparameter.

An important implementation detail concerns the handling of the cosine
annealing schedule across the phase boundary at epoch $\lfloor 0.3\,T \rfloor$.
Because Phase 2 begins with the activation of the adversarial objective, it
is important that the learning rate schedule for the generator optimizer is
correct at the start of Phase 2. The preferred embodiment achieves this by
initializing the scheduler with the \texttt{last\_epoch} parameter set to the
epoch count at the start of Phase 2, ensuring that the cosine annealing
formula is evaluated at the correct position in the schedule. Specifically,
when the scheduler is created at the start of Phase 2, the
\texttt{last\_epoch} is set to $\lfloor 0.3\,T \rfloor$ rather than $0$.

\subsection{Exponential Moving Average of Decoder Parameters}

\subsubsection{Motivation and Definition}

The exponential moving average (EMA) shadow parameter manager maintains a
parallel set of decoder parameters $\tilde{\btheta}$ that track the
time-averaged trajectory of the main decoder parameters $\btheta$ during
training. After each gradient update step, the EMA parameters are updated
according to:

\begin{equation}
  \tilde{\btheta}_t = \mu\,\tilde{\btheta}_{t-1} + (1 - \mu)\,\btheta_t,
  \label{eq:ema}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mu \in (0,1)$ is the EMA decay rate and $\btheta_t$ denotes
the main decoder parameters after the $t$-th gradient update. In the preferred
embodiment, $\mu = 0.9999$.

\subsubsection{Effect of EMA on Generative Quality}

The inventor has discovered that the EMA shadow parameters consistently produce
higher-quality synthetic images than the main training parameters at the end of
training, as measured by Fr\'{e}chet Inception Distance (FID)~\cite{heusel2017}.
The improvement arises because the stochastic gradient updates used in GAN
training impose high-frequency oscillations on the parameter trajectory, and
these oscillations prevent any single parameter vector along the trajectory from
achieving the performance of the time-average. The EMA, by averaging over many
steps of the trajectory, eliminates the high-frequency oscillations and produces
a parameter vector that more closely approximates the optimum of the training
objective.

The EMA is accumulated throughout all of Phase 2 training. In Phase 1, EMA
accumulation is not performed, as the reconstruction-only training of Phase 1
is more stable and the parameters at the end of Phase 1 are a reliable starting
point for Phase 2. EMA accumulation is activated at the start of Phase 2 and
continues until the end of training.

\subsubsection{Post-Training Weight Substitution}

At the end of training, the EMA shadow parameters $\tilde{\btheta}$ are
permanently substituted for the main decoder parameters $\btheta$. This
substitution is implemented by copying the EMA shadow parameters into the main
decoder parameter tensors, so that the resulting decoder model carries only a
single set of parameters and does not require any special handling during
inference. The substituted decoder is then saved as the production decoder
model. Referring to FIG.\ 9, this post-training substitution procedure is
illustrated schematically.

The permanent substitution approach ensures that inference-time image generation
uses the EMA-smoothed parameters without requiring any additional complexity in
the inference code. An alternative approach would be to maintain both sets of
parameters and select between them at inference time; the inventor has adopted
the substitution approach for its simplicity and to avoid the risk of
accidentally using the non-EMA parameters for inference.

\subsubsection{Numerical Considerations}

The inventor has observed that the EMA shadow parameters must be maintained at
full floating-point precision (FP32 or FP64) even when the main training
parameters are maintained at reduced precision (FP16 or BF16) for computational
efficiency. Accumulating EMA in reduced precision can cause the shadow
parameters to diverge from the true time-average due to rounding errors in
the accumulation step. In the preferred embodiment, the EMA shadow parameters
are always maintained at FP32 precision.

\subsection{Universal Dataset Interface}

\subsubsection{Convention-Over-Configuration Approach}

A distinguishing feature of the present invention is its universal dataset
interface, which allows the system to be applied to any image dataset organized
according to a standard folder hierarchy without any dataset-specific
configuration. The convention adopted is that the root dataset directory
contains one subdirectory per class, with the subdirectory name serving as the
class label, and each subdirectory contains the image files for that class.
Formally, the expected directory structure is:

\begin{center}
  \texttt{root/class\_0/*.jpg}\\
  \texttt{root/class\_1/*.jpg}\\
  \texttt{\quad \vdots}\\
  \texttt{root/class\_K/*.jpg}
\end{center}

This convention is identical to the \texttt{ImageFolder} convention used by the
PyTorch data loading library and is compatible with the vast majority of
publicly available image classification datasets.

\subsubsection{Automatic Class Discovery and Label Encoding}

Upon instantiation of the dataset loader, the universal interface automatically
enumerates all subdirectories of the root directory, assigns a unique integer
label to each class (in lexicographic order of class name), and constructs a
mapping from class name to integer label and from integer label to class name.
This enumeration is fully automatic and requires no user intervention. The
dataset loader then constructs a flat list of (image path, label) pairs spanning
all classes, from which training minibatches are drawn.

The automatic class discovery mechanism adapts to datasets with any number of
classes $K \geq 2$ and any class distribution, including severely imbalanced
distributions. The system correctly handles the case in which some classes have
many examples and others have very few, and automatically identifies which
classes require oversampling based on a user-specified target class size.

\subsubsection{Image Preprocessing Pipeline}

The universal dataset interface applies a standard image preprocessing pipeline
to each loaded image: the image is decoded from its on-disk format (JPEG, PNG,
or TIFF), resized to the target resolution $H \times W$ using bilinear
interpolation, converted to a floating-point tensor with values in $[0,1]$,
and normalized to zero mean and unit standard deviation using the per-channel
statistics of the ImageNet training set ($\mu = [0.485, 0.456, 0.406]$,
$\sigma = [0.229, 0.224, 0.225]$). This preprocessing pipeline is standard for
models pretrained on ImageNet, as it ensures compatibility between the input
distribution seen during ImageNet pretraining and the inputs provided during
fine-tuning. In the preferred embodiment, the target resolution is $128 \times
128$; in alternative embodiments, the target resolution can be any power of two
from $32 \times 32$ to $512 \times 512$.

\subsection{Checkpoint Protocol and Mid-Training Resume}

The training pipeline implements a checkpoint protocol that saves the complete
state of the training system to disk at the end of each epoch. The checkpoint
includes the main decoder parameters $\btheta$, the EMA shadow parameters
$\tilde{\btheta}$, the encoder parameters $\bphi$, the discriminator parameters
$\bpsi$, the optimizer states for all three optimizers, the scheduler states
for all schedulers, and the current epoch index and phase index. From this
checkpoint, training can be resumed seamlessly from the exact point at which
it was interrupted, including correct restoration of the learning rate schedule
and EMA shadow parameters.

The handling of the cosine annealing schedule during resume is an important
implementation detail. The PyTorch scheduler API uses a \texttt{last\_epoch}
parameter to determine the position in the cosine annealing curve; when
resuming from a checkpoint saved at epoch $t$, the scheduler must be
re-instantiated with \texttt{last\_epoch} set to $t - 1$ (since the scheduler
will call \texttt{step()} once at the start of the resumed epoch, advancing
to epoch $t$). Failure to restore the \texttt{last\_epoch} parameter correctly
results in the learning rate being reset to its initial value at the start of
the resumed training, which can cause severe instability if resuming occurs
deep into Phase 2 when the learning rate should be near its minimum.

\subsection{Alternative Embodiments}

\subsubsection{Alternative Perceptual Loss Functions}

In one alternative embodiment, the VGG-16 perceptual loss described in
Equation~(\ref{eq:perc}) may be replaced by a CLIP-based perceptual
loss~\cite{radford2021}, in which the perceptual similarity between the input
image and the reconstruction is measured in the feature space of a pretrained
CLIP visual encoder. The CLIP-based perceptual loss may be preferable in
applications where the input images are highly diverse and span domains not
well-represented in the VGG-16 training data. In yet another alternative
embodiment, both the VGG-16 and CLIP perceptual losses may be used jointly,
with a weighted combination analogous to Equation~(\ref{eq:recon}).

\subsubsection{Cross-Class SLERP for Extreme Imbalance}

In one alternative embodiment designed for the extreme imbalance regime in which
some minority classes have fewer than $k$ examples, the SLERP oversampling
module permits cross-class interpolation under controlled conditions. In this
alternative embodiment, when the within-class $k$-nearest-neighbor search fails
to find $k$ neighbors (because the class has fewer than $k$ examples), the
module falls back to a mixed interpolation strategy in which the nearest
neighbor is drawn from the same super-class (e.g., the same coarse category in
a hierarchical class taxonomy) rather than the same fine-grained class. The
interpolated embedding is then assigned the label of the source embedding
(the minority class example), not the label of the nearest-neighbor example.
This alternative embodiment extends the applicability of the method to datasets
with very small minority classes while maintaining label integrity.

\subsubsection{Online Synthesis During Training}

In one alternative embodiment, synthetic images are generated online during
training rather than in a separate post-training synthesis phase. In this
alternative embodiment, the SLERP oversampling module operates on a running
buffer of embeddings computed during the current training minibatch, and the
decoder generates synthetic images from the SLERP embeddings as part of each
training step. The synthetic images are included in the training minibatch as
augmented examples with their corresponding class labels. This online synthesis
approach allows the synthetic image distribution to adapt continuously as the
encoder and decoder improve, potentially providing higher-quality synthetic
images throughout training. However, it requires more careful management of the
training batch to avoid the synthetic images being used as targets for the
discriminator, which would create a circular training dependency.

\subsubsection{Multi-Scale Discriminator}

In one alternative embodiment, the single discriminator described herein is
replaced by a multi-scale discriminator comprising three or more
discriminator networks operating at different spatial resolutions (e.g.,
$128 \times 128$, $64 \times 64$, $32 \times 32$). Each discriminator network
produces a separate critic score, and the discriminator loss is the sum of the
individual critic losses. The multi-scale discriminator encourages the decoder
to generate realistic structures at multiple spatial scales simultaneously,
which may be beneficial for high-resolution synthesis tasks.

\subsubsection{Hardware and Parallelism}

The present invention is implemented on one or more computing devices comprising
central processing units (CPUs) and graphics processing units (GPUs). In the
preferred embodiment, all deep learning computations are performed on a single
GPU using the PyTorch deep learning framework. For large datasets requiring
longer training, the invention may be extended to multi-GPU data-parallel
training by distributing the training minibatch across multiple GPUs and
aggregating gradients using all-reduce operations. The WGAN-GP gradient penalty
computation must be performed synchronously per-GPU (before gradient
aggregation) to ensure that the gradient norms are computed with respect to the
correct device-local computation graph.

\subsection{Algorithm Pseudocode}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{SLERP-SMOTE Oversampling (Inference)}
\label{alg:slerp}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Trained encoder $E_{\bphi}$, training images $\{\bx_i, y_i\}_{i=1}^N$,
         EMA decoder $G_{\tilde{\btheta}}$, target count per class $N_{\text{target}}$,
         neighbors $k=5$
\State Compute embeddings: $\bz_i \leftarrow E_{\bphi}(\bx_i)$ for all $i$
\For{each class $c$ with $n_c < N_{\text{target}}$}
  \State $\mathcal{Z}_c \leftarrow \{\bz_i : y_i = c\}$
  \State $n_{\text{syn}} \leftarrow N_{\text{target}} - n_c$
  \For{$s = 1$ to $n_{\text{syn}}$}
    \State Sample anchor $\bz_0 \sim \mathcal{Z}_c$ uniformly at random
    \State Compute $k$-NN of $\bz_0$ in $\mathcal{Z}_c$ by cosine similarity
    \State Sample neighbor $\bz_1$ from the $k$-NN uniformly at random
    \State Compute $\omega \leftarrow \arccos(\bz_0 \cdot \bz_1)$
    \If{$\omega < 10^{-6}$}
      \State $t \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$
      \State $\hat{\bz} \leftarrow \text{normalize}((1-t)\bz_0 + t\bz_1)$
    \Else
      \State $t \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$
      \State $\hat{\bz} \leftarrow \frac{\sin((1-t)\omega)}{\sin\omega}\bz_0
              + \frac{\sin(t\omega)}{\sin\omega}\bz_1$
    \EndIf
    \State Compute class embedding $\bm{e}_c \leftarrow \mathbf{E}_c[c]$
    \State Generate synthetic image: $\hat{\bx} \leftarrow G_{\tilde{\btheta}}([\hat{\bz};\,\bm{e}_c])$
    \State Add $(\hat{\bx}, c)$ to the augmented training corpus
  \EndFor
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{End-to-End Training Procedure}
\label{alg:train}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Dataset $\mathcal{D}$, total epochs $T$, phase boundary $\tau = 0.3T$
\State Initialize $E_{\bphi}$, $G_{\btheta}$, $D_{\bpsi}$, EMA shadow $\tilde{\btheta} \leftarrow \btheta$
\State Initialize Adam optimizers $\text{opt}_{EG}$, $\text{opt}_D$
\State Initialize cosine schedulers with $T_{\max} = T$
\For{epoch $t = 0$ to $T-1$}
  \For{each minibatch $(\bx, y) \sim \mathcal{D}$}
    \State $\bz \leftarrow E_{\bphi}(\bx)$; $\bm{e}_y \leftarrow \mathbf{E}_c[y]$
    \State $\hat{\bx} \leftarrow G_{\btheta}([\bz;\,\bm{e}_y])$
    \State $\mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}} \leftarrow \lambda_{\text{L1}}\|\bx - \hat{\bx}\|_1 + \lambda_{\text{perc}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{perc}}(\bx, \hat{\bx})$
    \If{$t \geq \tau$} \Comment{Phase 2 only}
      \For{$n = 1$ to $n_{\text{disc}}$}
        \State Update $D_{\bpsi}$ with $\mathcal{L}_D$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:disc})
      \EndFor
      \State $\mathcal{L}_{\text{FM}} \leftarrow$ feature matching loss (Eq.~\ref{eq:fm})
      \State $\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} \leftarrow \lambda_G \mathcal{L}_G + \lambda_{\text{recon}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}} + \lambda_{\text{FM}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{FM}}$
    \Else
      \State $\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} \leftarrow \mathcal{L}_{\text{recon}}$
    \EndIf
    \State Update $E_{\bphi}, G_{\btheta}$ with $\nabla_{\bphi,\btheta}\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}}$
    \If{$t \geq \tau$}
      \State $\tilde{\btheta} \leftarrow \mu\,\tilde{\btheta} + (1-\mu)\,\btheta$ \Comment{EMA update}
    \EndIf
  \EndFor
  \State Step schedulers
  \State Save checkpoint
\EndFor
\State $\btheta \leftarrow \tilde{\btheta}$ \Comment{Permanent EMA substitution}
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Quality Assessment Metrics}

\subsubsection{Fr\'{e}chet Inception Distance}

The primary metric used to evaluate the quality of synthetic images in the
present invention is the Fr\'{e}chet Inception Distance
(FID)~\cite{heusel2017}, which measures the distance between the distribution
of real images and the distribution of synthetic images in the feature space
of a pretrained Inception-v3 network~\cite{szegedy2016}. The FID is computed
as:

\begin{equation}
  \text{FID} = \|\bm{\mu}_r - \bm{\mu}_s\|_2^2
    + \text{tr}\!\left(\bm{\Sigma}_r + \bm{\Sigma}_s
      - 2\bigl(\bm{\Sigma}_r \bm{\Sigma}_s\bigr)^{1/2}\right),
  \label{eq:fid}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $(\bm{\mu}_r, \bm{\Sigma}_r)$ and $(\bm{\mu}_s, \bm{\Sigma}_s)$
denote the mean and covariance of the Inception-v3 feature distribution for
real and synthetic images, respectively. Lower FID values indicate higher
quality and greater diversity of the synthetic images.

\subsubsection{Intra-Class FID}

In addition to the standard FID computed over all classes jointly, the present
invention computes an intra-class FID for each class separately. The intra-
class FID measures the quality and diversity of synthetic images within each
class independently, providing a more fine-grained assessment of the synthesis
quality that is particularly relevant for class-balancing applications where
the synthesis quality of each class is separately important.

\subsubsection{Downstream Classifier Performance}

The ultimate measure of the utility of the synthetic images is the performance
of a downstream image classifier trained on the augmented dataset (real images
plus synthetic images) relative to a classifier trained on the original
imbalanced dataset. The standard metrics for this comparison are per-class
recall, F1 score, and area under the receiver operating characteristic curve
(AUROC). An improvement in per-class recall for the minority classes, with
minimal degradation in majority-class accuracy, indicates that the synthetic
images provide genuine training benefit to the downstream classifier.

\section*{ABSTRACT}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

A system and method for generating photorealistic class-conditional synthetic
images to address class imbalance in image datasets. A convolutional neural
network encoder, augmented with an L2 normalization head that projects outputs
onto a unit hypersphere $\mathbb{S}^{D-1}$, maps training images to unit-norm
embedding vectors. A spherical oversampling module synthesizes new embedding
vectors by spherical linear interpolation (SLERP) along geodesic arcs
connecting $k$-nearest-neighbor pairs within each minority class, preserving
the unit-norm constraint and maintaining manifold fidelity. A class-conditional
DCGAN decoder, conditioned by concatenating a learned class embedding with the
image embedding, decodes each synthetic embedding to a synthetic image. The
decoder incorporates a non-local self-attention block at $16\times16$ spatial
resolution with $\gamma$-initialized-to-zero weighting. Encoder and decoder are
jointly trained through a phased schedule: reconstruction-only training for
the first thirty percent of epochs to establish a meaningful embedding
structure, followed by Wasserstein GAN training with gradient penalty and
feature matching loss for the remaining seventy percent. An exponential moving
average manager accumulates shadow decoder parameters with decay $\mu = 0.9999$
and permanently substitutes them for the main decoder parameters post-training.
A universal dataset interface ingests any folder-structured image dataset
without dataset-specific configuration. The resulting system generates
class-faithful synthetic images that demonstrably improve downstream classifier
performance on class-imbalanced datasets.
